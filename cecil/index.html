What Cecil Does

Cecil is the infrastructure layer for spatial intelligence. It makes spatial datasets — land use, flood risk, biodiversity, water stress, satellite imagery — accessible to data and science teams inside large enterprises, replacing a process that previously took months of procurement and specialist labor with a Python SDK call that takes minutes.

The product wraps dozens of datasets from providers like IBAT, WWF, and Planet into a unified API, standardized format, and shared scientific context layer. Enterprises subscribe once and pipe any dataset into their production notebooks and pipelines. Today Cecil's product looks like APIs, documentation, and metadata that fit into modern data workflows — tomorrow, it becomes the ontology layer that makes AI agents reliable when reasoning about the physical world.

The AlphaSense analogy is instructive: what began as a research tool for financial data grew into a $500M ARR platform used by 6,500+ customers by making data embedded, searchable, and API-accessible across the enterprise. Cecil is making the same bet on physical-world data — with the added tailwind that AI agents urgently need a scientific context layer to avoid silent critical errors.

Data team shift

AI context gap
Spatial datasets encode scientific meaning inconsistently — "forest" defined by canopy cover vs. land use vs. biodiversity; global forest models have only 26% overlap. Without a shared ontology, AI agents produce silent critical errors. One agent misestimated a carbon project by 10x. Cecil's context layer becomes essential AI infrastructure.

Customer concentration — Amazon is ~80%+ of meaningful revenue

Amazon is the only customer generating material revenue. Mitigants: Multiple Amazon teams are already using the platform organically (the Landbanking group, Taking Root, sustainability team). Amazon leadership has flagged budget for an internal app build. The product's Python SDK integration makes it sticky once embedded. But these are not contractual mitigants.

Runway — ~3.5 months of cash at current burn rate

$431K cash as of February 2026, with a burn rate of approximately $120–125K/month (Feb was elevated at $160K due to legal/tax fees). This gives roughly 3.5 months of runway — a very tight window.


The fundraise has launched (8+ meetings in the first week per Alex's March 2026 update) and appears to be gaining traction, but venture rounds can slip. Any deal taking 4+ months to close — not unusual for a seed round — could require bridge financing or operational cuts.


Mitigants: The team has demonstrated willingness to cut costs (let go of one hire in November 2025). There is also the possibility of a near-term Amazon enterprise agreement that would provide a significant cash injection. The fundraise is clearly the priority.

Platform vs. broker — is this durable infrastructure or a sophisticated middleman?
SEVERITY: MEDIUM IMPACT: HIGH ▼

The central long-term question for Cecil. Right now, Cecil's value is partly brokerage: it aggregates datasets, handles licensing, normalizes formats, and provides a unified API. This is genuinely valuable — but it's replicable in 2–3 years if data providers invest in their own API infrastructure, or if a hyperscaler (AWS, Snowflake, Google Cloud) builds a native spatial data marketplace.


The ontology/context layer is Cecil's claim to durable defensibility: a shared scientific context layer that makes datasets interoperable and makes AI workloads reliable. This is harder to replicate because it requires deep domain expertise (Tom Walker's PhD-level science team), active research collaboration, and community/standard-setting influence.


Key questions to probe: How mature is the ontology product today? Is it a shippable API feature or research work? What does Cecil's API response actually include in terms of structured scientific context? Are there any contractual relationships with data providers that create exclusivity or lock-in?

Gross margin structure — data pass-through constrains economics
SEVERITY: MEDIUM IMPACT: MEDIUM ▼

Current blended gross margins are approximately 35–45%, structurally constrained by data provider costs that are largely pass-through. In February 2026, gross margin was actually negative ($12K revenue, $13K cost of sales) because of a quarterly IBAT payment landing in the month.


The long-term margin story requires the platform fee (compute/API credits) and the context layer to become the dominant revenue driver. Data acquisition fees are a commodity moat — Cecil can charge modest margin on licensing and format normalization, but providers can always choose to go direct.


Mitigants: Platform fee as a % of total revenue has been growing (from zero in Q2 2025 to $5.8K in Jan 2026). Usage-based pricing means that as usage scales exponentially (Amazon-style), the platform fee grows much faster than data costs. The path to SaaS-like margins exists; it's just not demonstrated yet.

Sales motion — no repeatable enterprise GTM yet
SEVERITY: MEDIUM IMPACT: MEDIUM ▼

Cecil missed all three Q4 2025 revenue targets (100K data requests, $2.5M invoice, $500K Cecil revenue) despite enormous usage growth. The team explicitly acknowledged in the October update that they are "still learning the sales cycle."


Alex is currently running the entire commercial motion himself, supported by Connor Jordan in RevOps. Enterprise sales into Amazon, ING, MSCI, and PwC each requires a different buyer type (sustainability teams, data teams, procurement, compliance) and a different sales narrative. Without a dedicated enterprise sales leader, close rates and cycles will be inconsistent.


Mitigants: The consultant channel (Stantec, PwC) may be the unlock — each consultant relationship multiplies into many client relationships without needing separate enterprise sales motions. The product's bottom-up PLG nature means Cecil can get embedded at the data team level before ever needing to sell top-down.

Competitive displacement — Esri, AWS, or a well-funded startup
SEVERITY: LOW-MEDIUM IMPACT: HIGH ▼

Esri has 10M+ users and 700K+ customers — but it is built for a different buyer (GIS specialists, not data teams) and delivers data as reports and dashboards, not APIs. The incumbent moat is in the specialist workflow, not the production pipeline workflow Cecil is targeting.


Planet and Maxar are image providers, not infrastructure. They are more likely to be data providers on Cecil than competitors. AWS has data exchange products but has not built a spatial data context layer or ontology product.


The real competitive risk is a well-funded startup (with $5–20M) choosing to build in this space, or a hyperscaler deciding to build a native spatial data marketplace with their own licensing agreements. Neither has moved meaningfully as of April 2026.


Mitigants: Cecil's scientific depth and the active research publishing creates a credibility moat with data providers. Being embedded in Amazon's production pipeline creates switching cost. But the window to establish this moat is not unlimited.

Executive Summary
Cecil is building the infrastructure layer between fragmented spatial data providers and a growing class of enterprise users who need that data to power high-stakes, location-specific decisions. The product is a data marketplace and unified API platform that wraps datasets — land use, flood risk, biodiversity, water stress, satellite imagery — previously siloed in specialist GIS tools or buried behind complex licensing negotiations, into a standardized Python SDK and API that fits directly into data team notebooks and production pipelines.
The company launched its first enterprise customer (Amazon) in Q4 2025 and reached $100K+ in spend within three months — all from a single supply chain risk workflow.
