What Clair Does

Clair is building the world's first wearable continuous hormone monitor — a wrist-worn device that tracks estrogen, progesterone, LH, and FSH passively, without blood, urine, or saliva. The device uses a proprietary 10-biosensor stack and ML-driven modeling of the HPO axis to infer hormone levels in real time.

Today, understanding hormones requires blood tests months apart, costing $200–500 out of pocket. Existing wearables (Whoop, Oura, Apple Watch) ignore hormonal signals entirely. Clair creates an entirely new category — analogous to how CGMs created their own market beyond traditional glucose testing.

Primary demographics: fertility and perimenopause — both segments with zero continuous monitoring solutions available today.

Technology Architecture
0110-Sensor Biophysical Fusion
▶

Clair uses a 10-biosensor stack — versus 3–4 in most consumer wearables — including a non-multiplexed PPG sensor and custom-developed sensors. The core IP is a mathematical model of the HPO (hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian) axis using ML and differential equations to infer hormone levels from physiological signals.

Women's hormones are a homeodynamic system, not homeostatic — they require continuous monitoring to capture meaningfully. This is why point-in-time blood tests miss the picture.

02Why Not ISF Patches or Sweat Sensing
▶

Interstitial fluid (ISF) patches rejected: Hormone concentrations in ISF are 200,000–1,000,000× lower than glucose — far below reliable detection thresholds for wearable sensors.

Sweat electrochemical sensing rejected: Sweat degrades sensors rapidly, making this approach viable only in controlled lab environments. Not commercially viable as a continuous wear device.

03Clinical Validation Status
▶

In-house pilot study: 42 women, 252 cycles, 127 complete cycles. Results: 94% accuracy for cycle tracking (vs. Ava Fertility's 87%); MAE = 1.64 mIU/mL for LH; MAE = 2.07 µg/mL for PDG. Validated against urine-based at-home metabolite tests — the current at-home gold standard.

Blood serum study currently ongoing — comparing results to clinical blood draws. This is the decisive next validation milestone. Full technical whitepaper available under mNDA.

Research presented at the 2026 HHS National Conference on Women's Health.

04Regulatory Strategy
▶

Class 1 wellness device: Can launch immediately — no FDA clearance required. This is the Q4 2026 Clair 1.0 path.

Class 2 medical device: Pre-submission already filed with FDA. Clearance unlocks HSA/FSA eligibility and insurance reimbursement — a significant revenue expansion lever. VP of Quality (J&J, GE Healthcare) leads this process.

Risk Register
Critical  Blood Serum Study Results Unvalidated
▶

The pilot study (42 women, 252 cycles) was validated against urine-based at-home metabolite tests — the current consumer gold standard, not clinical blood draws. The ongoing blood serum study is the decisive scientific milestone. Until those results are available, the accuracy claims against actual hormone levels are unconfirmed.

Diligence question: Request technical whitepaper under mNDA immediately. What does the blood serum study timeline look like? What accuracy threshold is Clair targeting?

High  $169 vs. $369 Pricing Discrepancy
▶

Meeting notes reference a $169 device price. The deck prominently shows $369. These produce materially different unit economics: at $169, hardware gross margin drops to $132 (78%) vs. $332 (90%) at $369. LTV falls from $728 to ~$491 in the expected scenario. This is not a rounding difference — it changes the investment thesis.

Diligence question: Is $169 a launch price, a pre-order price, or an error in the notes? What is the actual go-to-market device price?

High  Q4 2026 Hardware Shipping Timeline
▶

Shipping a 10-sensor consumer wearable in approximately 6 months is aggressive — firmware and ML development are still ongoing. Hardware companies routinely miss ship dates by 6–12 months. A delay here puts 4,388+ pre-order customers at risk of churn and undermines the B2B MOU commitments.

Diligence question: Walk through the manufacturing ramp plan milestone by milestone. What is the DFM status? Has EVT/DVT/PVT been completed?

Medium  B2B MOU Conversion — Non-Binding
▶

The $20.6M B2B ARR headline is MOUs — memoranda of understanding, not signed purchase orders or contracts. Clinical partners have expressed intent to purchase, but intent at MOU stage has a meaningful falloff rate. Whether partners have budget allocated and what their procurement timelines look like is unknown.

Diligence question: What are the MOU terms? Binding or non-binding? Do partners have FY2026 budget allocated for device procurement? Australian distributor reference call.

Medium  Apple / Big Tech Competitive Entry
▶

Apple has the talent, clinical partnerships, FDA relationships, and distribution to enter women's hormonal health. A dedicated hormone sensor added to Apple Watch Series 13 would be existential for a standalone device. The moat Clair needs to build before that happens: clinical validation depth, FDA clearance, a branded women's health identity, and data moat from years of continuous monitoring.

Near-term, Whoop is the most-discussed competitive threat but our assessment is that their hardware and data position makes this 3–4 years away at minimum.

Medium  China Manufacturing / Tariff Exposure
▶

Primary manufacturing is in Shenzhen. India and Mexico facilities exist with the same tier-1 partner — providing geographic optionality — but the timeline and cost to shift primary volume is unknown. In the current tariff environment, this is a non-trivial risk for COGS.

Diligence question: What % of current production is Shenzhen? What is the timeline to shift primary production to India or Mexico? What is the COGS impact?

Medium  FDA Class 2 Pathway — Long and Expensive
▶

Class 1 wellness launch is ready and doesn't require FDA clearance. The Class 2 path (filed pre-submission) unlocks HSA/FSA eligibility and insurance reimbursement — potentially a major revenue and TAM expansion. But de novo clearance is 2–4 years, expensive, and uncertain. The model shouldn't rely on it for near-term projections.

Watch  Week-on-Week Traction Deceleration

Pre-orders this week (463) and signups (1,847) are both down meaningfully vs. last week (528 and 2,385). This is week 8 post-stealth — some normalization is expected. Demo day should re-accelerate. Clair Health is building the world's first wearable continuous hormone monitor — a wrist-worn device that tracks estrogen, progesterone, LH, and FSH without blood, urine, or saliva. The company is creating an entirely new category in the $100B+ women's health market, analogous to how CGMs created their own market beyond traditional glucose testing.

This is one of the strongest femtech signals we have seen at seed stage. We recommend proceeding to full diligence and moving quickly given the fundraise timeline.

The Opportunity

Women's health is at a structural inflection point. The wearable adoption rate among US women 18–45 has crossed 30%. Seventy-two percent of clinicians now say patients bring wearable or app data to appointments. New FDA guidelines from January 2026 are opening the regulatory window for continuous monitoring. The moment is now.

The primary markets — fertility (1 in 6 couples struggle to conceive) and perimenopause ($13B/year in symptom management spend) — currently have zero continuous hormone monitoring solutions available. The diagnostic gap is not incremental; it is categorical. Clair is not competing with existing wearables, it is creating a new category.

Beachhead: fertility and perimenopause consumers who are already wearable-literate and highly motivated. Expansion path: longevity, athletic performance optimization, post-menopausal cardiovascular monitoring, and clinical/insurance channels via FDA Class 2 clearance.

Product & Differentiation

The core technical differentiation is the 10-sensor biophysical fusion model — a mathematical representation of the HPO axis using ML and differential equations. This is not an incremental improvement on existing sensor stacks; it is a fundamentally different sensing philosophy. Existing wearables use 3–4 sensors explicitly trained to filter out hormonal signals as noise. Clair's algorithms treat those signals as the signal.

Interstitial fluid patches and sweat electrochemical sensing were both evaluated and rejected: hormone concentrations in ISF are 200,000–1,000,000× lower than glucose (below detection threshold), and sweat-based sensors degrade in commercial wear conditions. The biophysical modeling approach is the only viable non-invasive path.

The data moat is real and compounds: every cycle tracked improves model accuracy for that user and for the broader population model. This is not easily replicated by a new entrant starting from scratch.
